# Topic 2.3: Newton's Third Law

Newton's Third Law is often summarized by the famous adage: "For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction." While catchy, in AP Physics C, it is more precise to frame this in terms of interactions between objects.

## Definition
Newton's Third Law states that if object A exerts a force on object B ($\vec{F}_{A \to B}$ ), then object B exerts a force on object A ($\vec{F}_{B \to A}$) that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

Mathematically, this is expressed as:
$$ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A} $$

### Key Characteristics
It is vital to understand that these forces always exist in pairs, often called "action-reaction pairs." Note the following properties:
*   **Simultaneity:** The forces occur exactly at the same time.
*   **Different Objects:** The two forces in a third-law pair *never* act on the same object. This is a common point of confusion for students; if two forces act on the same object, they are not a third-law pair.
*   **Same Nature:** The forces are always of the same type (e.g., if one is a gravitational force, the other must be a gravitational force).

## Action-Reaction Pairs vs. Balanced Forces

Students frequently mistake balanced forces (from Newton's First Law) for third-law pairs. The table below clarifies the distinction:

| Feature | Third-Law Pair | Balanced Forces (Equilibrium) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Number of Objects** | Forces act on *different* objects. | Forces act on the *same* object. |
| **Sum of Forces** | Cannot be added (different objects). | Sum to zero ($\Sigma F = 0$). |
| **Requirement** | Always exist for any interaction. | Only occur in equilibrium. |
| **Example** | Earth pulling on ball, ball pulling on Earth. | Gravity on a table, Normal force on a table. |

## Why Objects Move
A common conceptual hurdle is: "If the forces are equal and opposite, why does anything move?"

Consider a horse pulling a cart. The horse pulls the cart forward with force $F_{H \to C}$, and the cart pulls the horse backward with force $F_{C \to H}$. If these forces are equal and opposite, shouldn't they cancel out? 

The answer is **no**, because they act on different bodies.
*   To determine the motion of the cart, you only look at forces acting *on the cart* (the horse's pull and friction).
*   To determine the motion of the horse, you look at forces acting *on the horse* (the cart's backward pull and the ground pushing the horse forward via friction).

To analyze systems effectively, ensure you understand [[Forces and Free-Body Diagrams]]. You must also be familiar with [[Newtons First Law]] and [[Newtons Second Law]] to synthesize how these interactions result in translational acceleration. 

## Summary
Newton's Third Law dictates the fundamental nature of force: forces are interactions between two objects. No object can exert a force on another without simultaneously experiencing an equal and opposite force back. This principle is foundational for understanding the motion of systems in classical mechanics.
