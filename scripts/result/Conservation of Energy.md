# Topic 3.4: Conservation of Energy

The principle of the **Conservation of Energy** is a fundamental pillar of physics. It states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another or transferred between systems. In an isolated system, the total energy remains constant.

## The Work-Energy Theorem
Before applying conservation, it is essential to understand how work changes energy. The [[Work]] done on an object by all forces equals the change in kinetic energy:

$$W_{net} = \Delta K = K_f - K_i$$

Where $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.

## Mechanical Energy
Mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic energy ($K$) and potential energy ($U$). The total mechanical energy of a system is defined as:

$$E_{mech} = K + U$$

### Conservative vs. Non-Conservative Forces
The conservation of energy depends heavily on the type of forces acting within a system.

| Force Type | Characteristics | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Conservative** | Work done is independent of the path taken; energy can be fully recovered. | Gravity, Ideal Spring force, Electric force |
| **Non-Conservative** | Work done depends on the path; energy is typically dissipated as heat or sound. | Friction, Air resistance, Tension, Applied forces |

[[Conservative Forces and Potential Energy]] explores the mathematical relationship between these forces and the potential energy function $U(x) = -\int F(x) dx$.

## The Conservation Principle
If only conservative forces act on a system, the total mechanical energy is conserved:

$$\Delta E_{mech} = \Delta K + \Delta U = 0$$

Or, written in terms of initial and final states:

$$K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f$$

### Dealing with Non-Conservative Forces
When non-conservative forces (like friction) are present, the total mechanical energy is not conserved because some energy is "lost" to the environment as thermal energy ($W_{nc}$). The extended work-energy equation becomes:

$$K_i + U_i + W_{nc} = K_f + U_f$$

Here, $W_{nc}$ represents the work done by non-conservative forces, which is negative if friction is acting opposite to the direction of motion.

## Problem Solving Strategy
To solve problems involving the conservation of energy, follow these steps:

1.  **Define the System:** Clearly identify what objects are included in your system.
2.  **Identify Forces:** Determine if the forces acting on the system are conservative or non-conservative.
3.  **Choose Reference Points:** Select a "zero point" for potential energy (e.g., the ground level for gravitational potential energy).
4.  **Set up the Equation:** Use the conservation equation if only conservative forces act, or the work-energy relation if external/non-conservative forces are involved.
5.  **Solve:** Algebraically isolate the variable of interest.

This topic is deeply related to other energy-based concepts, such as [[Power]] and the specific applications found in [[Rotational Kinetic Energy]]. Understanding how these forms of energy interact allows us to predict the motion of complex systems without needing to solve difficult differential equations of motion.
