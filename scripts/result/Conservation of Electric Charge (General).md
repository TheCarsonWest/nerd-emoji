# Conservation of Electric Charge (General)

The principle of the conservation of electric charge is one of the fundamental pillars of physics. It states that the net electric charge of an isolated system remains constant regardless of the changes taking place within the system. In any interaction, the total charge before the interaction must equal the total charge after the interaction.

## The Mathematical Expression

Mathematically, this principle is expressed as:

$$\sum q_{initial} = \sum q_{final}$$

This implies that while charge can be transferred between objects or redistributed within a system, it can never be created or destroyed. If a negative charge $-q$ is created in a process, a positive charge $+q$ must be created simultaneously, ensuring the net change is zero.

## Charge Neutrality in Physical Processes

Conservation of charge applies to both macroscopic and microscopic processes. Whether dealing with a simple [[Electric Charge and Electric Force]] problem involving spheres or complex subatomic particle physics, the rules remain the same.

### Particle Physics Examples
In high-energy physics, we often observe the creation and annihilation of particles. Even in these extreme environments, charge conservation is strictly maintained:

| Process | Reaction | Charge Balance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Beta Decay | $n^0 \rightarrow p^+ + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$ | $0 = (+1) + (-1) + 0$ |
| Pair Production | $\gamma \rightarrow e^+ + e^-$ | $0 = (+1) + (-1)$ |
| Annihilation | $e^+ + e^- \rightarrow \gamma + \gamma$ | $(+1) + (-1) = 0 + 0$ |

## Implications for Closed Systems

When we discuss the "General" conservation of charge, we must distinguish between closed and open systems.

1.  **Closed (Isolated) System:** No charge enters or leaves the system boundaries. The net charge is invariant.
2.  **Open System:** Charge can be added or removed through external wires or fields. In these cases, we must account for the flux of charge:
    $$\Delta Q_{sys} = Q_{in} - Q_{out}$$

This concept is vital when studying [[Electric Current]] and its relationship to flowing charges. When analyzing circuits, we often rely on the fact that charge cannot simply vanish inside a wire, leading to the development of [[Kirchhoff’s Junction Rule]].

## Limitations and Universality

The conservation of charge is considered an absolute symmetry in physics, known as a gauge symmetry. There is no experimental evidence suggesting that charge is not conserved. While quantum mechanics allows for the temporary creation of virtual particle pairs, the net charge of the universe remains constant.

Understanding this conservation law is essential before diving into more complex electromagnetic phenomena, such as how charge behaves in [[Electric Fields]] or how energy is stored in [[Capacitors]]. By mastering the general principle of charge conservation, you establish a reliable framework for solving problems ranging from electrostatics to complex circuit analysis.
