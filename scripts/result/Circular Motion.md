# Unit 2.10: Circular Motion

Uniform circular motion describes the movement of an object traveling at a constant speed along a circular path. Although the speed remains constant, the velocity vector is constantly changing direction, meaning the object is always accelerating. This acceleration is directed toward the center of the circle.

## Kinematics of Circular Motion

To analyze circular motion, we relate linear quantities to angular variables. For an object moving in a circle of radius $r$:

| Linear Quantity | Angular Quantity | Relationship |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Displacement ($s$) | Angle ($\theta$) | $s = r\theta$ |
| Velocity ($v$) | Angular Velocity ($\omega$) | $v = r\omega$ |
| Acceleration ($a_t$) | Angular Acceleration ($\alpha$) | $a_t = r\alpha$ |

*Note: For these formulas to hold, $\theta$ must be in radians.*

## Centripetal Acceleration

Even when an object moves at a constant speed, it undergoes acceleration because the direction of the velocity vector changes. This acceleration is called **centripetal acceleration** ($a_c$), directed radially inward toward the center of the path.

The magnitude of centripetal acceleration is defined by:
$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r$$

## Dynamics: Centripetal Force

According to [[Newtons Second Law]], acceleration requires a net force. In circular motion, the net force directed toward the center of the circle is the **centripetal force** ($F_c$).

$$F_c = ma_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

It is critical to remember that "centripetal force" is not a new type of fundamental force (like gravity or friction). It is a label for the net force directed toward the center. Depending on the scenario, the centripetal force is provided by tension, friction, gravity, or the normal force.

### Analyzing Forces in Circular Motion

When creating [[Forces and Free-Body Diagrams]] for circular motion problems, ensure the net force vector points directly toward the center of curvature.

1.  **Horizontal Circles (e.g., a car turning):** The force of static friction usually provides the necessary centripetal force.
2.  **Vertical Circles (e.g., a roller coaster loop):** The net force is the vector sum of gravity and normal force (or tension). At the top and bottom of the loop, the normal force must adjust to maintain the required centripetal acceleration.
3.  **Conical Pendulums:** The horizontal component of the tension force provides the centripetal acceleration.

## Period and Frequency

The motion of an object in a circle is periodic. The time it takes to complete one full revolution is the **period** ($T$). The number of revolutions per second is the **frequency** ($f$).

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}$$
