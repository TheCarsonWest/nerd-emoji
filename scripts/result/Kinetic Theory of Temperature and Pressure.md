# Topic 9.1: Kinetic Theory of Temperature and Pressure

The kinetic theory of gases provides a microscopic explanation for macroscopic properties like pressure and temperature. By modeling a gas as a collection of point-like particles in random motion, we can derive the relationship between molecular speed and the physical state of the system.

## Assumptions of the Kinetic Molecular Theory
To simplify the physics, we assume an **Ideal Gas** behaves according to the following postulates:
*   The gas consists of a large number of molecules in random, rapid motion.
*   The volume of the molecules themselves is negligible compared to the volume of the container.
*   Collisions between molecules and with the walls of the container are perfectly elastic.
*   There are no intermolecular forces (no attraction or repulsion) between molecules.

## Pressure and Molecular Motion
Pressure is defined as the force exerted by gas molecules colliding with the walls of a container. When a molecule hits a wall, its momentum changes, exerting a force according to Newton's Second Law.

The pressure $P$ exerted by an ideal gas can be derived as:
$$P = \frac{1}{3} \frac{N}{V} m \overline{v^2}$$

Where:
*   $N$ is the number of molecules
*   $V$ is the volume
*   $m$ is the mass of a single molecule
*   $\overline{v^2}$ is the mean square speed of the molecules

## Temperature and Kinetic Energy
In the kinetic theory, temperature is a direct measure of the average translational kinetic energy of the molecules. As temperature increases, the molecules move faster on average.

The average translational kinetic energy ($K_{avg}$) per molecule is given by:
$$\overline{K} = \frac{1}{2}m\overline{v^2} = \frac{3}{2}k_BT$$

Where $k_B$ is the Boltzmann constant ($1.38 \times 10^{-23} J/K$). This relationship bridges the gap between microscopic molecular motion and macroscopic temperature.

### Root-Mean-Square (RMS) Speed
Because individual molecules move at different speeds, we calculate the root-mean-square speed ($v_{rms}$) to represent the "typical" speed of a molecule:
$$v_{rms} = \sqrt{\overline{v^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{3k_BT}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}}$$

*   $R$ is the universal gas constant ($8.31 J/mol \cdot K$)
*   $M$ is the molar mass of the gas

## Summary Table of Kinetic Variables

| Variable | Definition | Relationship to Temperature |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pressure ($P$)** | Force per area exerted by collisions | Increases as $T$ increases |
| **Temperature ($T$)** | Measure of average kinetic energy | Proportional to average $\overline{K}$ |
| **RMS Speed ($v_{rms}$)** | Root-mean-square velocity | Proportional to $\sqrt{T}$ |

This kinetic interpretation is essential for understanding more complex thermodynamic processes found in [[The Ideal Gas Law]] and [[The First Law of Thermodynamics]]. For systems involving heat flow between these molecules, see [[Thermal Energy Transfer and Equilibrium]].
