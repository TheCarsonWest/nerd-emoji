# Topic 13.3: Refraction

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one transparent medium into another. This phenomenon occurs because the speed of light changes depending on the optical density of the material through which it is traveling. When light enters a medium where its speed is slower, it bends toward the normal line; conversely, when it enters a medium where it travels faster, it bends away from the normal.

## Index of Refraction

The "optical density" of a material is quantified by its index of refraction, denoted by $n$. It is a dimensionless constant defined as the ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum ($c$) to the speed of light in the material ($v$):

$$n = \frac{c}{v}$$

Because light always travels slower in a medium than in a vacuum ($v < c$), the index of refraction is always greater than or equal to 1. 

| Material | Index of Refraction ($n$) |
| :--- | :--- |
| Vacuum | 1.000 |
| Air | 1.0003 |
| Water | 1.33 |
| Glass (Crown) | 1.52 |
| Diamond | 2.42 |

## Snell's Law

The quantitative relationship describing how light bends is known as **Snell's Law**. It relates the indices of refraction of the two media and the angles of incidence and refraction, measured with respect to the "normal" (a line perpendicular to the surface at the point of incidence).

$$n_1 \sin(\theta_1) = n_2 \sin(\theta_2)$$

Where:
*   $n_1$ and $n_2$ are the indices of refraction of the incident and refracting media, respectively.
*   $\theta_1$ is the angle of incidence.
*   $\theta_2$ is the angle of refraction.

## Conceptual Implications

When analyzing refraction problems, consider these two scenarios based on the relationship between $n_1$ and $n_2$:

1.  **Traveling into a more dense medium ($n_2 > n_1$):** The light ray slows down and bends **toward** the normal line ($\theta_2 < \theta_1$).
2.  **Traveling into a less dense medium ($n_2 < n_1$):** The light ray speeds up and bends **away** from the normal line ($\theta_2 > \theta_1$).

## Wavelength and Frequency

It is a crucial point in physics that while the speed of light changes upon refraction, the **frequency ($f$) of the light remains constant** because it is determined by the source. Consequently, since $v = f\lambda$, if the speed changes, the wavelength must also change according to the relationship:

$$\lambda_n = \frac{\lambda_0}{n}$$

Where $\lambda_0$ is the wavelength in a vacuum and $\lambda_n$ is the wavelength in the medium.
