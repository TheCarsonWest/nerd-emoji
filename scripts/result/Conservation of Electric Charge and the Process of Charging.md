# Topic 8.2: Conservation of Electric Charge and the Process of Charging

## Conservation of Electric Charge
The law of conservation of charge states that the total electric charge in an isolated system remains constant over time. Charge is neither created nor destroyed; it is merely transferred between objects. When subatomic particles are created (e.g., in high-energy particle collisions), they are always created in pairs with equal and opposite charges (e.g., an electron and a positron), ensuring the net charge remains zero.

The quantization of charge is a fundamental principle, expressed as:
$$q = ne$$
Where:
*   $q$ is the total charge on an object.
*   $n$ is an integer (positive or negative).
*   $e$ is the elementary charge, approximately $1.602 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}$.

## The Process of Charging
Charging an object involves the movement of electrons from one object to another. Protons are bound tightly within the nucleus and generally do not move during common electrostatic charging processes. There are three primary methods of charging:

### 1. Charging by Friction
When two neutral, dissimilar materials are rubbed together, electrons are transferred from one material to the other due to differences in electron affinity. One object becomes positively charged (lost electrons), while the other becomes negatively charged (gained electrons).

### 2. Charging by Conduction
This involves physical contact between a charged object and a neutral object. Electrons redistribute themselves to achieve equilibrium. The final charge is shared between the two objects. If the objects are identical in size and shape, the charge will divide equally.

### 3. Charging by Induction
Charging by induction allows an object to be charged without physical contact.

| Method | Contact Required? | Result |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Friction** | Yes | Objects gain opposite charges. |
| **Conduction** | Yes | Objects gain the same sign of charge. |
| **Induction** | No | Neutral object gains opposite charge of the inductor. |

## Conductors vs. Insulators
The ability of an object to hold or transfer charge depends on its material properties.

*   **Conductors:** Materials (like metals) where outer electrons are loosely bound and free to move throughout the material. If charge is placed on a conductor, it spreads out rapidly over the entire surface.
*   **Insulators:** Materials (like glass, rubber, or plastic) where electrons are tightly bound to atoms. Charge placed on an insulator remains localized where it was placed.

## Summary of Key Concepts
1.  **Net Charge:** Always conserved in an isolated system.
2.  **Electron Flow:** In most macroscopic processes, only electrons (negative charge) move.
3.  **Equilibrium:** Charges in a conductor will redistribute until the electric field inside the material is zero.
