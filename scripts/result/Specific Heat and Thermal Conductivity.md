# 9.5: Specific Heat and Thermal Conductivity

Understanding how materials react to heat and how that heat moves through them is fundamental to thermodynamics. This section explores heat capacity and the mechanisms of heat conduction.

## Specific Heat Capacity

When heat is added to a substance, its temperature generally rises. However, the amount of heat required to change the temperature of a given mass varies by material. This property is known as specific heat capacity ($c$).

The relationship between heat added ($Q$), mass ($m$), change in temperature ($\Delta T$), and specific heat capacity is given by:

$$Q = mc\Delta T$$

Where:
*   $Q$ is the thermal energy added or removed (Joules, J).
*   $m$ is the mass of the substance (kg).
*   $c$ is the specific heat capacity ($J/kg \cdot K$).
*   $\Delta T$ is the change in temperature ($K$ or $^\circ C$).

### Molar Heat Capacity
Sometimes it is more convenient to express heat capacity in terms of moles ($n$) rather than mass. This uses the molar heat capacity ($C$):

$$Q = nC\Delta T$$

## Thermal Conductivity

Thermal conductivity describes the rate at which heat energy flows through a material due to a temperature gradient. Unlike specific heat, which is about energy storage, conductivity is about energy transport.

Consider a rod of material with cross-sectional area $A$ and length $L$, with one end at temperature $T_H$ (hot) and the other at $T_C$ (cold). The rate of heat flow, or power ($P = \frac{dQ}{dt}$), is given by:

$$P = \frac{k A (T_H - T_C)}{L}$$

Where:
*   $P$ is the rate of heat transfer (Watts, W).
*   $k$ is the thermal conductivity of the material ($W/m \cdot K$).
*   $A$ is the cross-sectional area ($m^2$).
*   $L$ is the thickness or length of the material ($m$).
*   $(T_H - T_C)$ is the temperature difference across the material.

### Material Properties Comparison

| Material | Thermal Conductivity ($k$ in $W/m \cdot K$) | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Copper | ~400 | Excellent conductor |
| Steel | ~50 | Common alloy |
| Glass | ~0.8 | Poor conductor (Insulator) |
| Air | ~0.026 | Very poor conductor |

## Key Concepts and Connections

Materials with high thermal conductivity are typically good electrical conductors as well, due to the presence of free electrons that aid in transporting both charge and kinetic energy. Conversely, insulating materials, such as wood or plastic, have very low values of $k$.
