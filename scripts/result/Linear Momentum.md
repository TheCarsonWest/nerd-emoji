# Topic 4.1: Linear Momentum

Linear momentum is a fundamental concept in classical mechanics that describes the "quantity of motion" an object possesses. It relates an object's mass to its velocity and provides a pathway to understanding collisions, explosions, and other dynamic interactions between systems.

## Defining Linear Momentum

Linear momentum, denoted by the vector symbol $\vec{p}$, is defined as the product of an object's mass $m$ and its velocity $\vec{v}$. Because velocity is a vector, momentum is also a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

The mathematical definition is:

$$\vec{p}=m\vec{v}$$

### Key Properties

| Property | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Vector Nature** | Momentum points in the same direction as the velocity vector. |
| **SI Units** | Measured in kilogram-meters per second ($kg \cdot m/s$). |
| **Additivity** | The total momentum of a system is the vector sum of the momenta of individual particles. |

For a system of $n$ particles, the total momentum $\vec{P}_{total}$ is:

$$\vec{P}_{total}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\vec{p}_i=\vec{p}_1+\vec{p}_2+...+\vec{p}_n$$

## Momentum and Newton's Second Law

Historically, Newton formulated his second law not as $\vec{F}=m\vec{a}$, but in terms of momentum. He stated that the net external force acting on an object is equal to the rate of change of its momentum with respect to time.

$$\vec{F}_{net}=\frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$$

If we assume the mass of the object is constant, this expression simplifies to the familiar $\vec{F}_{net}=m\vec{a}$. However, the definition involving the derivative is more powerful because it remains valid even when mass changes over time (such as in a rocket expelling fuel).

## Relationship to Impulse

Momentum is intrinsically linked to the concept of [[Change in Momentum and Impulse]]. When an external force acts on an object over a period of time, it causes a change in the object's momentum. This change is known as impulse.

If you are analyzing how momentum changes during a collision or interaction, it is often easier to calculate the impulse rather than the instantaneous force.

## Conservation of Momentum

One of the most important principles in physics is the [[Conservation of Linear Momentum]]. This principle states that if the net external force on a system is zero, the total momentum of that system remains constant, regardless of the internal interactions occurring within the system.

This concept is essential for solving problems involving:
*   [[Elastic and Inelastic Collisions]]
*   Explosions and separation events
*   Recoil scenarios

By applying this conservation law, we can often determine the velocities of objects after a collision without needing to know the specific forces involved during the impact.
