# 12.4: Ampère's Law

## Overview
Ampère's Law provides a powerful, symmetric method to calculate magnetic fields generated by steady currents, analogous to how [[Gausss Law]] is used to calculate electric fields from charge distributions. When a current distribution possesses high symmetry (such as cylindrical or solenoid symmetry), Ampère's Law is significantly more efficient than using the [[Magnetic Fields of Current-Carrying Wires and the Biot-Savart Law]].

## The Mathematical Formulation
Ampère's Law states that the line integral of the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ around any closed loop (an Amperian loop) is directly proportional to the total current enclosed by that loop.

The equation is defined as:
$$\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{enclosed}$$

### Variables Defined
| Variable | Definition | Units (SI) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $\oint$ | Closed line integral (around the Amperian loop) | N/A |
| $\vec{B}$ | Magnetic field vector | Tesla (T) |
| $d\vec{l}$ | Infinitesimal vector element along the loop | Meters (m) |
| $\mu_0$ | Permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7}$) | $T \cdot m/A$ |
| $I_{enclosed}$ | Net current passing through the surface of the loop | Amperes (A) |

## Strategy for Application
To solve problems using Ampère's Law effectively, follow this systematic approach:

1.  **Identify Symmetry:** Ensure the current distribution has enough symmetry (cylindrical, planar, or solenoidal) so that the magnitude of $\vec{B}$ is constant over portions of the loop.
2.  **Choose an Amperian Loop:** Select a closed path that reflects the symmetry of the source. The vector $\vec{B}$ should either be parallel or perpendicular to $d\vec{l}$ at every point on the loop.
3.  **Calculate $I_{enclosed}$:** Determine the net current passing through the surface bounded by your chosen loop. Remember to account for direction using the right-hand rule.
4.  **Evaluate the Integral:** Simplify $\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l}$. Usually, this results in $B \times (\text{Length of path})$.
5.  **Solve for B:** Algebraic manipulation will yield the magnitude of the magnetic field.

## Important Applications

### 1. Long Straight Wire
For a long straight wire carrying current $I$, we construct a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$. Due to circular symmetry, $B$ is uniform.
$$\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I$$
$$B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$$

### 2. Solenoids
A solenoid is a long coil of wire. For an ideal solenoid, the magnetic field inside is uniform and the field outside is approximately zero. Using a rectangular Amperian loop that spans across the edge of the solenoid, we can derive the field:
$$B = \mu_0 n I$$
Where $n$ represents the number of turns per unit length ($N/L$).

## Limitations
Ampère's Law in this form is only valid for **magnetostatics** (steady currents where $\frac{d\vec{E}}{dt} = 0$). If the electric field changes over time, we must use the generalized version known as the Ampère-Maxwell Law, which includes the displacement current term. For a deeper understanding of dynamic fields, see [[Electromagnetic Induction]].
