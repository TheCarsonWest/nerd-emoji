# AP Physics C: Magnetic Fields

Magnetic fields represent a fundamental force in electromagnetism. Unlike electric fields, which originate from stationary charges, magnetic fields are generated by moving charges (currents) and intrinsic magnetic dipoles.

## Defining the Magnetic Field ($\vec{B}$)

A magnetic field, denoted by $\vec{B}$, is a vector field that exerts a force on moving charges or currents. The SI unit for magnetic field strength is the Tesla ($T$), where $1 T = 1 \frac{N}{C \cdot m/s} = 1 \frac{N}{A \cdot m}$. Another common unit is the Gauss ($G$), where $1 T = 10^4 G$.

### Properties of Magnetic Field Lines
Magnetic field lines describe the shape and strength of the magnetic field.
*   **Direction:** The field lines point in the direction a North pole of a compass would point.
*   **Continuity:** Field lines never start or end; they form continuous closed loops. They exit the North pole and enter the South pole of a magnet.
*   **Density:** The density of the field lines is proportional to the magnitude of the magnetic field; where lines are closer together, the field is stronger.

## Magnetic Force on a Moving Charge

The primary way we detect and measure magnetic fields is through the force they exert on a charged particle. The magnitude and direction are determined by the Lorentz force law (specifically the magnetic component):

$$\vec{F}_B = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$$

Where:
*   $q$ is the charge of the particle.
*   $\vec{v}$ is the velocity vector.
*   $\vec{B}$ is the magnetic field vector.

### The Cross Product and Right-Hand Rule
Because the force involves a cross product, the resulting force vector $\vec{F}_B$ is always perpendicular to both $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$. The magnitude is given by:

$$F_B = |q|vB\sin(\theta)$$

Where $\theta$ is the angle between the velocity vector and the magnetic field vector.

| Angle ($\theta$) | $\sin(\theta)$ | Effect on Force |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $0^\circ$ or $180^\circ$ | 0 | No magnetic force exerted |
| $90^\circ$ | 1 | Maximum magnetic force |

*Note: For negative charges, the force direction determined by the Right-Hand Rule must be reversed.*

## Key Concepts and Relationships

*   **Work Done by Magnetic Fields:** Because the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity vector ($\vec{F}_B \perp \vec{v}$), the magnetic force does **zero work** on a moving charge. Consequently, a magnetic field can change the direction of a particle's motion, but it cannot change its kinetic energy or speed.
*   **Motion in a Uniform Field:** When a charge enters a uniform magnetic field perpendicular to its velocity, it undergoes uniform circular motion. The magnetic force acts as the centripetal force:
    $$F_B = F_c \implies qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
