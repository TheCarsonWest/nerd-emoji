# Topic 9.3: Thermal Energy Transfer and Equilibrium

Thermal energy, often referred to as internal energy, describes the microscopic kinetic and potential energy of the particles within a system. When two systems at different temperatures are in contact, thermal energy transfers between them until they reach a state of thermal equilibrium.

## Thermal Equilibrium
Thermal equilibrium occurs when two systems in thermal contact no longer exchange heat, meaning they have reached the same temperature. This is the foundational concept for the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics, which states that if system A is in thermal equilibrium with system B, and system B is in equilibrium with system C, then A and C are in equilibrium with each other. This allows us to define temperature as a property that dictates the direction of heat flow (from high temperature to low temperature).

## Mechanisms of Thermal Energy Transfer
Heat ($Q$) can be transferred through three primary mechanisms: conduction, convection, and radiation.

### 1. Conduction
Conduction is the transfer of energy through direct contact of matter. Energy is transferred via molecular collisions, where higher-energy molecules transfer kinetic energy to their neighbors. 
*   **Key variables:** Material properties (thermal conductivity), cross-sectional area, temperature gradient.
*   [[Specific Heat and Thermal Conductivity]]

### 2. Convection
Convection involves the transfer of energy via the bulk movement of a fluid (liquid or gas). As a fluid heats up, it expands and becomes less dense, causing it to rise while cooler, denser fluid sinks to replace it. This creates a circulation pattern known as a convection current.

### 3. Radiation
Radiation is the transfer of energy via electromagnetic waves. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation does not require a material medium. All objects with a temperature above absolute zero emit thermal radiation.

## Energy Transfer Formulas

The following table summarizes how we quantify energy transfer in different contexts:

| Mechanism | Governing Principle / Equation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Conduction** | $$P = \frac{kA\Delta T}{L}$$ |
| **Specific Heat** | $$Q = mc\Delta T$$ |
| **Phase Change** | $$Q = mL$$ |

Where:
*   $P$ is the power (rate of heat transfer, $J/s$).
*   $k$ is the thermal conductivity of the material ($W/m \cdot K$).
*   $A$ is the cross-sectional area ($m^2$).
*   $\Delta T$ is the temperature difference ($K$ or $^\circ C$).
*   $L$ is the thickness of the material ($m$).
*   $c$ is the specific heat capacity ($J/kg \cdot K$).
*   $L$ (in phase change) is the latent heat of transformation ($J/kg$).

## Thermal Equilibrium Calculations
When solving problems involving two substances of different temperatures interacting in an isolated system, we apply the principle of conservation of energy. Since the system is isolated, the heat lost by the warmer object must equal the heat gained by the cooler object:

$$\sum Q = 0$$

Or more intuitively:

$$Q_{lost} + Q_{gained} = 0$$
$$-(m_1c_1(T_f - T_1)) = m_2c_2(T_f - T_2)$$

Here, $T_f$ represents the final equilibrium temperature. Always ensure your units for specific heat and mass are consistent across the equation.
