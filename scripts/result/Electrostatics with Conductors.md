# Electrostatics with Conductors

In electrostatics, a conductor is a material where charge carriers (typically electrons) are free to move throughout the material. This mobility creates specific, fundamental properties when the conductor is in electrostatic equilibrium—meaning the charges are at rest and no net macroscopic motion occurs.

## Key Properties of Conductors in Equilibrium

When a conductor reaches electrostatic equilibrium, the following rules apply:

1.  **Internal Electric Field is Zero:** If there were a non-zero electric field inside a conductor, the free charges would experience a force ($F=qE$) and would continue to move. Since the definition of equilibrium is that charges are not moving, the net electric field inside the material must be zero.
    $$E_{inside} = 0$$

2.  **Excess Charge Resides on the Surface:** Since $E=0$ inside the conductor, according to [[Gausss Law]], any closed Gaussian surface drawn inside the material must enclose a net charge of zero. Therefore, any excess charge placed on a conductor must migrate to its outer surface.

3.  **Electric Field at the Surface:** The electric field just outside the surface of a charged conductor must be perpendicular to that surface. If there were a tangential component, the charges on the surface would move along the surface, violating equilibrium. The magnitude is related to the surface charge density ($\sigma$):
    $$E_{surface} = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0}$$

4.  **Conductor is an Equipotential Volume:** Because the electric field is zero everywhere inside the conductor, there is no work required to move a charge between any two points within or on the surface of the conductor. Thus, the entire conductor is at the same [[Electric Potential]].

## Summary Table

| Property | Value in Electrostatic Equilibrium |
| :--- | :--- |
| Net Electric Field ($E$) inside | $0$ |
| Excess Charge Location | Surface only |
| Electric Field just outside surface | $\frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0}$ (perpendicular to surface) |
| Electric Potential ($V$) | Constant throughout the conductor |

## The Hollow Conductor and Shielding

If a conductor has a hollow cavity, the electric field inside that cavity is also zero, provided no charges are placed within the cavity itself. This phenomenon is known as **Electrostatic Shielding** (often called a Faraday Cage). 

If a charge $q$ is placed inside the cavity of a hollow conductor, the inner surface of the conductor will induce a charge of $-q$ to ensure the electric field within the conducting material remains zero. Consequently, a charge of $+q$ will appear on the outer surface of the conductor to conserve charge.

## Electric Fields near Points

While the potential is constant, the charge distribution on the surface depends on the shape of the conductor. Charge tends to accumulate at sharp points (areas with small radii of curvature). At these points, the surface charge density $\sigma$ is much higher, resulting in a significantly stronger electric field ($E = \sigma / \epsilon_0$) near sharp points compared to flat regions. This is the operating principle behind lightning rods.
