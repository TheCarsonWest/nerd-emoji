# Unit 2.6: Gravitational Force

Gravitational force is a fundamental interaction in the universe, defined as an attractive force that acts between any two objects with mass. In AP Physics C, we move beyond the simple $F_g = mg$ used in introductory courses to understand the universal nature of gravity.

## Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation

Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers. This is defined by:

$$F_g = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

### Key Variables and Constants
| Symbol | Definition | SI Units |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $F_g$ | Gravitational Force | Newtons (N) |
| $G$ | Universal Gravitational Constant | $6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{kg}^2$ |
| $m_1, m_2$ | Masses of the two objects | Kilograms (kg) |
| $r$ | Distance between centers of mass | Meters (m) |

## Gravitational Field Strength
While $F_g$ describes the force between two specific objects, the gravitational field strength ($g$) describes the acceleration an object would experience at a specific point in space due to a primary mass ($M$).

The field strength is defined as the force per unit mass:
$$g = \frac{F_g}{m} = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$

This relationship explains why acceleration due to gravity decreases as you move further away from the center of a planet. Note that this concept is essential for understanding [[Motion of Orbiting Satellites]].

## Important Conceptual Notes
*   **Vector Nature:** Gravity is a vector quantity. It always points directly toward the center of the mass creating the field. When dealing with systems of multiple masses, use the principle of superposition to sum the individual gravitational force vectors.
*   **Action-Reaction Pairs:** Because the gravitational force is an interaction between two bodies, it obeys [[Newtons Third Law]]. The force exerted by mass 1 on mass 2 is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the force exerted by mass 2 on mass 1.
*   **Center of Mass:** For spherical objects, we treat the entire mass as being concentrated at the center. This is a crucial simplification derived from calculus.

## Weight vs. Mass
It is vital to distinguish between weight and mass. Mass is an intrinsic property of matter representing inertia, whereas weight is the force of gravity acting on that mass. 

If you are standing on the surface of a planet, your weight is simply the gravitational force between you and the planet. If you move away from the surface (into orbit), your mass remains constant, but your weight (the force pulling you toward the planet) decreases according to the inverse-square law.
